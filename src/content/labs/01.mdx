<p class="promise">
  You will not leave with a demo. You will leave with a public, FDE-style case study: a triage agent,
  a deterministic eval run against 40 public labels, and a field report that shows how you scope,
  build, measure, and explain.
</p>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>The customer problem</h2>
  <blockquote class="story">
    <p>
      A regional bank receives hundreds of consumer complaints every week. The operations team needs
      to route each one to the right internal team and catch the complaints that carry regulatory,
      legal, or customer-harm risk.
    </p>
    <p>
      Today, routing is inconsistent. Some complaints go to the wrong team. Some high-risk complaints
      get treated like routine service issues. And some complaints sound severe because of tone, but
      the underlying issue is operationally routine. Separating those is the job.
    </p>
  </blockquote>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>What this actually is</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      A fixed-list triage challenge against a frozen snapshot of real consumer complaint narratives,
      derived from the CFPB Consumer Complaint Database. You build an AI agent that makes exactly two
      scored calls per complaint: which team it routes to, and whether it carries a risk flag. Every
      decision must cite verbatim evidence from the narrative, and the evaluator checks that each
      quote actually appears in the text.
    </p>
    <p>
      This is not live monitoring, not a hidden benchmark, and not a leaderboard. The 40 labels are
      public by design. The point is evaluation discipline, evidence grounding, and failure analysis
      under a customer-shaped constraint, the same skills FDE and applied AI teams run on.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>The dataset</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      200 real complaint narratives, published by the CFPB with consumer opt-in and scrubbed of
      personal information before publication. The narratives are messy on purpose: redaction
      markers (XXXX), typos, all-caps passages, rambling structure. The mess is the challenge, not a
      bug to clean.
    </p>
    <p>
      Decisions come only from the cached record. If the evidence is not in the record, it does not
      exist for this challenge. No outside knowledge about companies or regulators, no live browsing.
      Treat redacted content as unknown.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>The six routing teams</h2>
  <CardRow items={teams} />
  <p class="passbar-lead">Tiebreak rule:</p>
  <ul class="passbar">
    <li>route by the primary subject matter, not by severity</li>
    <li>severity is captured by the risk flag, not the route</li>
    <li>a fraud complaint with regulatory exposure routes to fraud_disputes with risk_flag true</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>The risk flag</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      Flag a complaint when the record suggests serious potential harm or regulatory concern:
      repeated failure to resolve a fraud claim, inability to access essential funds, foreclosure or
      severe financial loss, discrimination or fair-lending concerns, privacy misuse, or a company
      allegedly ignoring required dispute processes.
    </p>
  </div>
  <p class="bp">
    <strong>Calibration rule one.</strong> Angry tone alone is not high risk. An emotional complaint
    can be operationally routine.
  </p>
  <p class="bp">
    <strong>Calibration rule two.</strong> A short narrative is not automatically low risk. A short
    complaint can still describe fraud, loss of funds, discrimination, or inability to access money.
  </p>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>The bar</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      40 hand-labeled complaints with public labels, spread across all six teams and seeded with the
      hard cases: angry-but-routine, short-but-severe, multi-issue, vague, and heavily redacted
      narratives. A deterministic evaluator scores your committed predictions against them. Reruns
      verify, they do not regenerate.
    </p>
  </div>
  <BarTable headers={metricHeaders} rows={metricRows} />
  <p class="passbar-lead">Grading rules:</p>
  <ul class="passbar">
    <li>an agent that flags everything gets perfect recall and terrible precision, so precision is reported alongside recall</li>
    <li>every evidence quote is validated against the narrative after normalization</li>
    <li>a flagged row with no valid evidence is called out by the evaluator</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>How it runs</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      Self-serve, in one afternoon. Clone the repo, run the reference agent, score it, then make it
      yours: change the prompts, the decomposition, the evidence selection, or the whole approach.
      The agent is provider-agnostic with Anthropic as the default. AI must do the core reasoning;
      deterministic code handles orchestration, validation, and scoring.
    </p>
  </div>
  <pre class="cmd">{quickstart}</pre>
  <ol class="runlist">
    <li>Run the reference agent and the evaluator</li>
    <li>Read your failures against the 40 labels</li>
    <li>Change the approach and rerun</li>
    <li>Write the field report from the template</li>
    <li>Submit a GitHub issue linking your fork and committed predictions</li>
  </ol>
</section>

<section class="sec">
  <h2>Two ways to submit</h2>
  <div class="col">
    <p>
      <strong>Mini report, 30 to 60 minutes.</strong> Run the reference agent, change one thing,
      report your score and one failure you understood.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Full field report, 2 to 4 hours.</strong> Your own approach, a decision log with
      tradeoffs, a failure table with root causes, and what you would change in v2. This is the
      portfolio artifact.
    </p>
    <p>
      Anyone can rerun the evaluator against your committed predictions. That is the proof: not that
      your agent was perfect, but that you measured it honestly and understood where it broke.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>
